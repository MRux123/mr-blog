Operation Epic Fury: The US-Israel Strike on Iran

By Michał Rukszan Updated: March 8, 2026, 12:09 AM CET (Warsaw)

February 2026 will go down in history. On the final day of the month, the USA and Israel launched a massive joint military operation against Iran, reshaping global geopolitics in a single moment. Earlier in the month, the world had been captivated by the Super Bowl and the Winter Olympic Games.

World — Turning Point: US & Israel Attack Iran (28 February)

On 28 February 2026, the USA and Israel launched a joint military operation. Israel named it Operation 'Lion's Roar', while the USA called it Operation 'Epic Fury'. President Donald Trump announced the start of a 'serious war operation' with the stated goal of destroying Iran's nuclear and ballistic missile programmes. Approximately 200 Israeli fighter jets struck nearly 500 targets across Iran, marking the largest aerial operation in Israel's history.

Explosions rocked Tehran and dozens of cities; Iranian airspace was closed, and mobile communications were disrupted across multiple regions. Supreme Leader of Iran Ali Khamenei was killed, a death confirmed by multiple media outlets. In retaliation, Iran announced the closure of the Strait of Hormuz—posing a direct threat to 20% of global oil and gas supplies. Furthermore, Iran retaliated with strikes on Israel and US bases; explosions were reported in Dubai and Doha, and Kuwait and UAE airports were struck.

An emergency UN Security Council session was convened at the request of China and Russia, with Beijing and Moscow heavily condemning the attack. Additionally, Russia pledged support for Iran and shared intelligence data with Tehran.

Background — Escalation in Preceding Months

The escalation was driven by several factors in the preceding months. Protests in Iran since 28 December 2025, fueled by the collapse of the rial and surging public anger, accelerated the decision to strike. Israel had originally planned the operation for mid-2026 but brought the date forward due to the protest dynamics and stalled nuclear talks. Negotiations in Oman and Geneva in February 2026 yielded no agreement on Iran's nuclear programme. On 19 February, Trump warned: "I give it at most 10–15 days before bad things happen." By 27 February, the IAEA was unable to verify whether Iran had suspended uranium enrichment.

Other Key Events

Before the geopolitical crisis, global attention was focused on entertainment and sports. On 8 February, Super Bowl LX took place at Levi's Stadium in Santa Clara, with Bad Bunny headlining the halftime show and drawing record-breaking viewership figures. Concurrently, the Winter Olympics 2026 in Milan and Cortina generated global excitement, with Polish athletes claiming medals.

Poland: Response to the Iran Strike

In response to the Middle East crisis, Prime Minister Donald Tusk confirmed that Polish citizens and embassy staff in Tehran were safe, assuring the public that Poland was prepared for multiple scenarios. LOT Polish Airlines and other carriers immediately suspended flights to the Middle East region as airspace over Iran and the Gulf states was closed. Diplomatically, Foreign Minister Sikorski successfully intervened to secure the release of five Polish nationals previously detained in Iraq, after the Iraqi Foreign Ministry denied espionage charges.

Poland: Domestic Politics

On the domestic front, TV Republika recorded its first significant ratings decline in a long time, allowing TVP1 to return to the top of the audience rankings. Meanwhile, debate continued around the SAFE programme (savings fund) as President Nawrocki weighed a veto or signature. The Civic Coalition (KO) also announced changes to the KPO reform agenda, dropping one reform and adding a Poland 2050 proposal.

Economy & Technology

The economic impact of the military operation was severe and immediate. The Strait of Hormuz closure caused global oil prices to spike sharply, gripping financial markets in uncertainty. The USA assembled its largest military force in the Middle East since the 2003 Iraq War, leading to a sharp rise in US military expenditure. Meanwhile, in Poland, the NBP Monetary Policy Council cut interest rates to 3.75%, continuing the trend of monetary policy normalisation.
